**60 Questions with Explanations, Topic Codes, and Skill Codes**

Each entry shows the correct answer, a short explanation of the reasoning, the CED topic code, and the CED skill code. Use this to identify which specific topics need more review, not just whether the question was right or wrong.

## Unit 1: Thinking Geographically

**Q1: B — Environmental determinism**
The argument that climate *inevitably* dictates cultural traits is the textbook definition of environmental determinism, an early 20th-century view now widely rejected. Possibilism (the modern alternative) emphasizes that the environment sets limits but humans choose among many possibilities.
*Topic 1.5 | Skill 1.A*

**Q2: B — Patterns visible at one scale may obscure variation at another**
A national average smooths over wide local differences. This is a classic scale of analysis problem and a common AP exam target.
*Topic 1.6 | Skill 5.A*

**Q3: B — Smaller scales may diverge from larger-scale aggregates**
The state and national numbers look strong, but the city of Cleveland is much lower. The aggregated number hides the local pattern.
*Topic 1.6 | Skill 5.C*

**Q4: B — Mercator distorts size near the poles**
Greenland appears nearly the size of Africa on a Mercator map; in reality, Africa is about 14 times larger. Mercator preserves direction (which is why it's good for navigation) but distorts area badly at high latitudes.
*Topic 1.1 | Skill 4.A*

**Q5: C — Equal-area projection**
For comparing area (like deforestation), use an equal-area projection so that landmasses are shown in their true relative size. Robinson is a reasonable compromise but does not preserve area exactly.
*Topic 1.1 | Skill 4.A*

**Q6: B — GIS incorporating remote sensing**
GIS layers multiple types of spatial data (vegetation imagery, tax records, zoning) for combined analysis. Remote sensing is one input; GIS is the broader system that integrates them.
*Topic 1.2 | Skill 4.A*

**Q7: C — Friction of distance and resulting distance decay**
The friction of distance is the underlying cause; distance decay is the observed pattern. Both terms describe the same phenomenon at different levels of explanation, which is why C is more complete than B.
*Topic 1.3 | Skill 1.D*

**Q8: C — Perceptual (vernacular) region**
The Bible Belt has no official boundary. It is defined by shared cultural perception, which makes it vernacular.
*Topic 1.4 | Skill 1.A*

**Q9: B — Possibilism**
Iceland's climate is hostile to tomato growing, but technology overcomes that limit. Humans work within environmental constraints rather than being determined by them.
*Topic 1.5 | Skill 1.B*

**Q10: A — Aggregated data at larger scales can mask variation at smaller scales**
Metro median is $78K, but Hough is $24K. Same idea as Q2 and Q3, just framed with income data. Scale of analysis is heavily tested, so worth seeing repeatedly.
*Topic 1.6 | Skill 5.A*

**Q11: C — "The Midwest"**
"The Midwest" has no fixed legal boundary; people disagree on which states it includes. That informal, perception-based definition makes it vernacular. The EU is formal, the newspaper zone is functional, and the Mississippi watershed is formal/physical.
*Topic 1.4 | Skill 1.E*

**Q12: B — Situation**
Site is the absolute physical characteristics of a place (the harbor itself). Situation is the place's position relative to other places (along the shipping lanes).
*Topic 1.3 | Skill 1.A*

**Q13: B — Choropleth map**
Choropleth maps shade enumeration units (like counties) by a value, usually a rate or percentage. They are the standard for "percent by county" data.
*Topic 1.1 | Skill 4.A*

**Q14: B — National-scale interventions can produce uneven outcomes across regions**
Rural Appalachia and urban Boston have very different economic structures, so the same policy will produce different results. This is the scale-of-analysis idea applied to policy. Skill 5 (Scale Analysis) shows up on the FRQ, so practice this framing.
*Topic 1.6 | Skill 5.B*

**Q15: B — Time-space convergence**
Travel times between two fixed places shrink as transportation technology improves. Places effectively grow "closer" in time even though physical distance is unchanged.
*Topic 1.3 | Skill 2.A*

## Unit 2: Population and Migration

**Q16: C — Stage 3**
Stage 3 of the demographic transition: birth rates falling, death rates already low, growth slowing. Stage 5 (sub-replacement) would mean birth rates have fallen *below* death rates.
*Topic 2.5 | Skill 3.C*

**Q17: B — Tax credits and parental leave**
Pronatalist policies *encourage* births. Examples include cash bonuses, tax breaks, and parental leave. The other choices are antinatalist.
*Topic 2.7 | Skill 5.B*

**Q18: B — Push: civil war / Pull: jobs**
Push factors drive people *out* of an origin (war, persecution, drought). Pull factors draw them *toward* a destination (jobs, safety, family). A is the choices reversed.
*Topic 2.10 | Skill 2.A*

**Q19: B — Sub-Saharan country in Stage 2 with high natural increase**
High CBR (38), low CDR (9), and a very young age structure (43% under 15) match Stage 2 demographics typical of many Sub-Saharan African countries today.
*Topic 2.4 | Skill 5.B*

**Q20: B — Stage 2 with high fertility**
A wide base = lots of children = high fertility. A narrowing top = high mortality at older ages. Symmetrical sides rule out heavy male out-migration.
*Topic 2.3 | Skill 3.D*

**Q21: B — Refugees (forced migration)**
Crossing an international border to flee violence makes them refugees. IDPs would still be inside Syria.
*Topic 2.11 | Skill 2.A*

## Unit 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes

**Q22: B — Blending into a stable new language with native speakers**
Creoles arise when pidgins (temporary trade languages) become the native language of a community, often in colonial contact zones (Haitian Creole, Louisiana Creole, etc.).
*Topic 3.5 | Skill 1.A*

**Q23: B — Cultural syncretism**
Syncretism is the blending of two or more cultural or religious traditions into a new combined form. Día de los Muertos is the textbook example.
*Topic 3.8 | Skill 2.B*

**Q24: B — Placemaking**
Placemaking is the deliberate effort by communities to give a public space identity, character, and meaning. Murals, benches, and markets are classic examples.
*Topic 3.3 | Skill 4.B*

**Q25: B — Universalizing seek converts globally; ethnic are tied to a specific people/place**
Universalizing: Christianity, Islam, Buddhism. Ethnic: Judaism, Hinduism, traditional/folk religions. The active pursuit of converts is the defining contrast.
*Topic 3.7 | Skill 2.D*

**Q26: B — The use of chopsticks for eating**
A culture trait is a single attribute (a tool, a practice, a belief). The whole set of traits is "culture." A region of shared traits is a culture region.
*Topic 3.1 | Skill 1.A*

**Q27: B — British colonization and U.S. influence**
Lingua francas spread through historical processes of empire, trade, and economic dominance, not because they are easy or officially designated.
*Topic 3.6 | Skill 1.D*

**Q28: B — Cultural landscape**
A cultural landscape is the visible imprint of human activity on the land. The church, grain elevator, and memorial together tell the story of who lived there and what they valued.
*Topic 3.2 | Skill 4.D*

## Unit 4: Political Patterns and Processes

**Q29: B — Boundaries match a population sharing identity, language, history**
A nation-state ideally has a single nation (cultural group) within a single state (political unit). Iceland comes close because of its small, relatively homogeneous population.
*Topic 4.1 | Skill 1.A*

**Q30: B — Region caught between competing external powers**
Shatterbelts (the Balkans, parts of Eastern Europe historically, the Caucasus) experience fragmentation and conflict because of pressure from multiple outside powers.
*Topic 4.3 | Skill 5.B*

**Q31: C — Fragmented**
Fragmented states are split into pieces, usually islands or separated land areas. Indonesia and the Philippines are textbook cases.
*Topic 4.5 | Skill 4.A*

**Q32: B — Concentrating voters into a few districts**
Packing concentrates the target group so they "waste" votes by winning a few districts overwhelmingly. Cracking (option A) is the opposite, splitting them across many districts to dilute their power.
*Topic 4.6 | Skill 2.C*

**Q33: B — Power constitutionally divided between central and subnational governments**
Federal states: U.S., Germany, Brazil, India, Canada, Australia. Unitary states: France, Japan, China.
*Topic 4.7 | Skill 1.A*

**Q34: C — Superimposed boundary**
Superimposed boundaries are drawn by an outside power (often after war) without regard to existing cultural or physical patterns. Antecedent: drawn before settlement. Subsequent: drawn after, accommodating culture. Relict: no longer functioning.
*Topic 4.4 | Skill 4.A*

**Q35: B — Devolution**
Devolution is the transfer of powers from a central government to subnational regions while keeping the country intact. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland are the standard UK examples.
*Topic 4.8 | Skill 1.D*

**Q36: B — Cede certain powers to a supranational organization in exchange for benefits**
Supranationalism involves *some* transfer of authority, not total surrender. Member states keep their governments and most sovereignty.
*Topic 4.9 | Skill 1.B*

## Unit 5: Agriculture and Rural Land Use

**Q37: B — Population would outstrip food supply**
Malthus's core claim was that geometric population growth would always outrun arithmetic food production growth, leading to famine, war, or disease as natural checks.
*Topic 5.6 | Skill 1.E*

**Q38: A — Population pressure drives innovation and intensification**
Boserup essentially flipped Malthus: when population grows, people invent new techniques (irrigation, multi-cropping, fertilizers) to expand food output. This is why Malthus's prediction has not held in many places.
*Topic 5.4 | Skill 1.E*

**Q39: D — Intensive horticulture and dairying**
Perishable, high-value goods locate close to market because they spoil and transport costs matter most. The classic ring order in Von Thünen: intensive farming/dairy → forestry → grains → ranching at the periphery.
*Topic 5.8 | Skill 4.E*

**Q40: B — High-yielding seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, irrigation**
Norman Borlaug's wheat varieties and the introduction of IR8 rice are the iconic examples. Asia and Latin America saw the largest gains; Sub-Saharan Africa less so.
*Topic 5.5 | Skill 1.D*

**Q41: B — Commodity dependency**
When a country's economy leans heavily on a small number of primary-sector exports (cocoa, oil, minerals), it is vulnerable to global price swings. This is a common pattern in former colonial economies.
*Topic 5.9 | Skill 1.B*

**Q42: B — Extensive commercial agriculture**
Lots of land, few workers, low output per acre, single grain crop = extensive (large-scale grain or ranching). Intensive farms are the opposite: small land, many workers, high yields per acre.
*Topic 5.1 | Skill 4.A*

**Q43: B — Tropical and subtropical highlands**
Coffee needs warm temperatures, consistent rainfall, and elevation. The "coffee belt" runs through Latin America, equatorial Africa, and parts of Southeast Asia.
*Topic 5.1 | Skill 4.A*

**Q44: A — Choropleth map by county**
Same logic as Q13. A rate or percentage by enumeration unit (county) calls for a choropleth.
*Topic 5.1 | Skill 4.F*

**Q45: B — Globalization of agriculture**
Strawberries from California in summer, Mexico in winter, Chile in our spring, all in the same supermarket. Refrigerated supply chains and air freight make it possible.
*Topic 5.10 | Skill 5.B*

**Q46: B — 18th–19th century mechanization, crop rotation, selective breeding**
First Agricultural Revolution: ~10,000 years ago, domestication. Second: industrial-era farming improvements. Third (Green Revolution): mid-20th century high-yield seeds.
*Topic 5.3 | Skill 1.D*

## Unit 6: Cities and Urban Land Use

**Q47: B — Maximum distance consumers will travel**
Range = how far people will travel for the good. Threshold = minimum customer base needed to support it. Higher-order goods (luxury cars, specialty hospitals) have larger ranges and thresholds.
*Topic 6.4 | Skill 2.A*

**Q48: B — Highest near the CBD, declining outward**
The bid-rent curve: businesses outbid one another for central, high-accessibility land. Land values fall with distance from the CBD, with secondary peaks at suburban nodes.
*Topic 6.5 | Skill 2.A*

**Q49: B — Primate city pattern**
The largest city is more than twice the size of the second largest (12.0 vs. 1.5, an 8x ratio). That dominance defines a primate city. Rank-size rule would put City B around 6 million, City C around 4 million, etc.
*Topic 6.4 | Skill 1.C*

**Q50: B — Commercial spine and elite sector with disamenity zones at the periphery**
Griffin-Ford features a wealthy spine running outward from the CBD, with poorer zones farther out and disamenity zones (informal settlements, often called *favelas* or *barrios*) on the edge. Note this is the inverse of the U.S. concentric zone pattern in many ways.
*Topic 6.5 | Skill 1.E*

**Q51: B — Urban sustainability**
Solar panels reduce a city's carbon footprint and reliance on fossil-fuel grids. This is a textbook urban sustainability example.
*Topic 6.11 | Skill 1.E*

## Unit 7: Industrial and Economic Development

**Q52: B — HDI combines health, education, and income**
The three HDI dimensions: long and healthy life (life expectancy), knowledge (schooling), and standard of living (GNI per capita). It is broader than GDP alone.
*Topic 7.3 | Skill 3.A*

**Q53: B — Country Q likely has a higher GII**
Country Q's worse maternal mortality, higher adolescent birth rate, lower female political representation, and lower female labor force participation all push GII upward. Higher GII = more inequality.
*Topic 7.3 | Skill 3.C*

**Q54: B — Rapid growth in leading sectors, manufacturing, rising investment**
Rostow's five stages: traditional society → preconditions for take-off → take-off → drive to maturity → age of mass consumption. Take-off is the inflection point where industrialization accelerates.
*Topic 7.4 | Skill 1.A*

**Q55: B — Intermediate position with traits of both**
Wallerstein: core (wealthy industrial states), periphery (raw-materials economies), semi-periphery (Mexico, Brazil, China historically). Semi-periphery acts as a buffer and exhibits mixed characteristics.
*Topic 7.5 | Skill 1.A*

**Q56: B — Transportation, labor, agglomeration**
Weber's three factors. Transportation cost depends on weight-loss vs. weight-gain inputs. Labor cost can pull firms away from the transport-optimal site. Agglomeration is the benefit of clustering with similar firms.
*Topic 7.6 | Skill 1.A*

**Q57: A — Deindustrialization and post-industrial transition**
The shift from primary/secondary employment to tertiary/quaternary is the classic post-industrial pattern. Same trajectory the U.S., U.K., and most Western European economies have followed.
*Topic 7.7 | Skill 5.D*

**Q58: B — Debt forgiven in exchange for conservation commitments**
Debt-for-nature swaps trade financial relief for environmental protection (rainforest preservation, marine reserves). Used in Costa Rica, Bolivia, Ecuador, and others.
*Topic 7.8 | Skill 2.D*

**Q59: B — Spatial inequality and uneven development**
The classic core-periphery pattern within a single country. Coastal China, southeast Brazil, and northern Italy all show this internal unevenness.
*Topic 7.1 | Skill 3.B*

**Q60: B — Comparative advantage**
David Ricardo's principle: countries gain from trade by specializing in what they produce at lower opportunity cost, even if another country can produce everything more cheaply in absolute terms.
*Topic 7.2 | Skill 1.B*

## Scoring Guide

Count the number correct out of 60.

| Raw Score | Approximate Implied Performance |
|---|---|
| 50–60 | Strong, on track for a 4 or 5 if FRQs are solid |
| 40–49 | Solid, on track for a 3 or 4 |
| 30–39 | Working knowledge with real gaps; targeted review needed |
| Below 30 | Foundational review across multiple units |

The actual AP exam combines a 60-question MC section (50% of score) with 3 FRQs (50%). This practice set covers only the MC half, so treat the score as an indicator, not a final prediction.

## How to Review

1. **Sort the misses by unit and topic code** to spot clusters. A single wrong answer in Unit 1 is noise; three or four in Unit 1 is a signal.
2. **Check the skill code** on misses too. Repeated errors on Skill 5.x mean scale analysis needs work, regardless of which unit the question came from.
3. **Read the explanation even on correct answers** if she wasn't sure. Lucky guesses don't carry forward.
