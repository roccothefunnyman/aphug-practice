## Unit 1: Thinking Geographically

**Q1. B.** The Mercator projection preserves direction and local shape, which is useful for navigation, but distorts area, especially at high latitudes (Greenland appears far larger than it is). Topic 1.1. Skill 4.A.

**Q2. A.** The bilingualism rate looks low at the national scale (14%) but very high at the local scale (93%). The pattern shows why national averages can mask sharp local differences, which is the heart of scale-of-analysis thinking. Topic 1.6. Skill 5.A.

**Q3. C.** Environmental determinism is the discredited idea that physical environment, especially climate, directly determines economic and cultural outcomes. Possibilism replaced it with the view that environment sets limits but humans choose responses. Topic 1.5. Skill 1.A.

**Q4. B.** Functional regions are defined by interaction with a node (here, the radio station). Formal regions share a uniform trait, and perceptual regions exist in people's minds. Topic 1.7. Skill 1.A.

**Q5. C.** Spatial patterns can look very different at different scales of analysis. A national average can hide localized concentrations, which is exactly the kind of question that gets missed when scale framing is overlooked. Topic 1.6. Skill 5.A.

**Q6. B.** Distance decay is the decline in interaction or influence with increasing distance. Phone calls between cities falling off with distance is a classic example. Topic 1.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q7. B.** Equal-area projections preserve relative area but distort shape and direction, the opposite tradeoff from Mercator. Topic 1.1. Skill 4.A.

**Q8. B.** Layering and overlaying spatial datasets to support decisions is a core function of GIS. Remote sensing provides imagery; mental maps are cognitive, not analytical. Topic 1.2. Skill 3.A.

**Q9. B.** Possibilism holds that the environment limits but does not determine human activity, leaving room for cultural and technological choice. Topic 1.5. Skill 1.A.

**Q10. C.** A choropleth showing income by county represents a single theme using shaded areas, which makes it a thematic map. Reference maps show general features; topographic maps show elevation. Topic 1.1. Skill 4.A.

**Q11. C.** This is a classic scale-of-analysis result: the dominant explanatory variable shifts depending on the scale you study. None of the levels is "correct" on its own; together they show a fuller picture. Topic 1.6. Skill 5.B.

**Q12. B.** Arithmetic density divides population by total land area; physiological density divides by arable land. A wide gap between the two means most land is not farmable, concentrating people on a small productive area. Topic 1.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q13. B.** The Bible Belt has no formal boundary or governing body. It exists mainly through shared perception of cultural and religious identity, which is the definition of a perceptual or vernacular region. Topic 1.7. Skill 1.A.

**Q14. C.** Site refers to physical characteristics of a location. Situation refers to its position relative to other places, transport networks, and economic features. Topic 1.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q15. B.** Cartographic scale is about how a map represents the world (the ratio of map distance to ground distance). Scale of analysis is about the level at which a phenomenon is studied (global, national, local). They share a word but mean different things, and confusing them is a common AP miss. Topic 1.6. Skill 5.A.

## Unit 2: Population and Migration

**Q16. B.** A wide-base pyramid with high birth rates and falling death rates is the textbook profile for Stage 2 of the demographic transition. Stage 1 has both rates high; Stage 4 has both low; Stage 5 (where used) shows shrinkage. Topic 2.5. Skill 4.B.

**Q17. B.** A push factor drives people away from their origin. Drought destroying crops is a clear push. The relative's job offer would be a pull factor. Topic 2.10. Skill 1.A.

**Q18. A.** Country P has 42% under age 15, which produces a high youth dependency ratio of about 78% (42/54). That places real pressure on schools, pediatric services, and family supports. B is plainly wrong (Country P, with 42% under 15, has the younger population). C ignores that the 100% sums to total population, not dependency burden. D is wrong because Stage 4 has a low share of children, not 42%. Topic 2.3. Skill 3.B.

**Q19. B.** Ravenstein's laws emphasize that most migration is short-distance and step-wise, with longer-distance moves typically directed at major centers of economic opportunity. Topic 2.10. Skill 1.B.

**Q20. C.** Forced migration involves coercion by a state or other power. Voluntary migrants choose to move; chain migration follows family or community networks. Topic 2.11. Skill 1.A.

**Q21. A.** A national pyramid that looks balanced can hide very different regional structures, with one region still in Stage 2 and another already in Stage 5. This is scale of analysis applied to demography. Topic 2.5. Skill 5.A.

## Unit 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes

**Q22. B.** Creolization occurs when a contact language stabilizes and is acquired natively by the next generation. Tok Pisin in Papua New Guinea is a clear case. Topic 3.7. Skill 1.A.

**Q23. C.** Syncretism is the blending of two or more cultural or religious traditions into a new, distinct form. Santería pairs Yoruba Orishas with Catholic saints, which fits the definition cleanly. Topic 3.8. Skill 1.A.

**Q24. B.** Placemaking is the deliberate shaping of public space to build identity, belonging, and use by the community. Murals, markets, plantings, and renaming all qualify. Topic 3.2. Skill 1.A.

**Q25. C.** Hierarchical diffusion spreads from larger or more influential places to smaller ones (big cities to small cities to towns). Contagious diffusion would spread outward to everyone in proximity. Topic 3.4. Skill 1.B.

**Q26. A.** Christianity is universalizing (it actively seeks converts across cultures). Hinduism is ethnic (closely tied to a specific cultural and regional context). The other pairings reverse or scramble these categories. Topic 3.3. Skill 1.A.

**Q27. B.** Visible elements like shrines, signage, and architectural features make up the cultural landscape, which reflects local cultural traits regardless of every resident's individual beliefs. Topic 3.2. Skill 4.A.

**Q28. B.** A neighborhood concentrated around shared ethnic identity, language, and institutions is the textbook definition of an ethnic enclave. Topic 3.3. Skill 1.A.

## Unit 4: Political Patterns and Processes

**Q29. B.** A nation-state requires close alignment between a culturally cohesive nation and the state's borders. Iceland is a strong example, with relative cultural and linguistic homogeneity within stable borders. The U.S., Russia, and Belgium are all multinational. Topic 4.3. Skill 1.A.

**Q30. B.** Belgium contains two large linguistic-cultural communities (Flemish and Walloon) with distinct identities and substantial regional autonomy, which makes it a multinational state. Topic 4.3. Skill 1.A.

**Q31. C.** A superimposed boundary is drawn by an outside power across an existing cultural landscape, with little regard for the people already living there. The post-WWI Sykes-Picot lines and the broader colonial partition of the Middle East are textbook examples. Antecedent boundaries predate dense settlement; subsequent boundaries are negotiated to fit existing cultural patterns; relic boundaries no longer function as borders. Topic 4.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q32. B.** Antecedent boundaries predate dense settlement and shape later cultural patterns. Subsequent boundaries are drawn after settlement; superimposed boundaries are forced over existing patterns; relic boundaries no longer function but leave traces. Topic 4.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q33. B.** Packing concentrates opposition voters into a single district so they win one race by a large margin and lose surrounding ones. Cracking does the opposite: splitting opposition voters across many districts to dilute their influence. Topic 4.7. Skill 4.B.

**Q34. C.** Devolution is the transfer of power from a central government to subnational units. The Scottish Parliament is a frequently cited modern example. Topic 4.8. Skill 1.A.

**Q35. B.** Supranational organizations involve member states giving up some sovereignty to a higher governing body for shared benefit, as with shared currency, trade rules, and free movement. Topic 4.9. Skill 1.A.

**Q36. C.** Fragmented states are made up of separated pieces of territory, often islands or non-contiguous regions. The Philippines, with thousands of islands, fits clearly. Topic 4.4. Skill 1.A.

## Unit 5: Agriculture and Rural Land Use

**Q37. C.** In Von Thünen's model, perishable, high-value goods like dairy and market gardening locate closest to the city because transport costs and spoilage matter most for them. Topic 5.10. Skill 4.B.

**Q38. B.** Refrigerated transport, global supply chains, and modern logistics weaken the link between physical distance to market and the type of land use, which is one of Von Thünen's core assumptions. Topic 5.10. Skill 1.B.

**Q39. B.** Malthus argued that population grows geometrically (exponentially) while food supply grows arithmetically, leading to crisis unless population is checked by famine, disease, or restraint. Topic 2.6. Skill 1.A.

**Q40. C.** Boserup pushed back on Malthus, arguing that population pressure spurs innovation and intensification in agriculture, expanding what the land can support. Topic 5.11. Skill 1.A.

**Q41. B.** The mid-20th-century Green Revolution introduced high-yield seed varieties (notably wheat and rice), synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation, especially in parts of South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Latin America. Topic 5.11. Skill 1.A.

**Q42. A.** Country V draws more than 80% of its exports from three primary commodities, which is the textbook profile of commodity dependency. Price swings in coffee, bananas, or sugar will hit it hard, while Country W's diversified exports buffer it. Topic 5.12. Skill 3.B.

**Q43. B.** The First (Neolithic) Agricultural Revolution involved the domestication of plants and animals and the shift toward settled farming, which gradually replaced hunting-gathering as the primary food strategy in many regions. Topic 5.2. Skill 1.A.

**Q44. B.** The Second Agricultural Revolution accompanied the Industrial Revolution. Crop rotation, selective breeding, mechanization, and improved tools raised yields and freed labor for urban industrial work. Topic 5.2. Skill 1.A.

**Q45. B.** The defining difference is who the output is for: commercial agriculture sells into markets, while subsistence agriculture feeds the producing household and community. The other contrasts in the answer choices are not reliable. Topic 5.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q46. A.** Bid-rent and Von Thünen-style logic both predict that land use intensity and rents fall with distance from market. Seeing the same pattern across regions at the same scale of analysis reinforces the theory's applicability. Topic 5.10. Skill 5.A.

## Unit 6: Cities and Urban Land Use

**Q47. B.** Burgess described urban form as concentric rings around a central business district, with each ring representing a different land use and social profile, drawn from observations of early-20th-century Chicago. Topic 6.6. Skill 1.A.

**Q48. B.** Hoyt's sector model argued that urban land use forms wedge-shaped sectors radiating outward, often along transport corridors, rather than uniform rings. Topic 6.6. Skill 1.B.

**Q49. B.** Harris and Ullman observed that real cities often grow around several specialized centers (industrial, retail, residential), with compatible activities clustering and incompatible ones repelling each other. Topic 6.6. Skill 1.B.

**Q50. B.** The Griffin-Ford model of Latin American cities centers on a CBD with a commercial spine and elite residential corridor extending outward, ringed by zones of progressively lower-quality housing and disamenity sectors at the periphery. Topic 6.6. Skill 1.B.

**Q51. B.** A primate city is disproportionately larger than the next-largest city in its country (often more than twice as large). The 12 million versus 1.5 million gap is far above what the rank-size rule would predict, indicating primacy. Topic 6.3. Skill 1.B.

## Unit 7: Industrial and Economic Development

**Q52. B.** Weber's least cost theory treats optimal industrial location as the result of minimizing transportation costs, labor costs, and the pull of agglomeration (clustering with related firms). Topic 7.3. Skill 1.A.

**Q53. B.** A long shift out of agriculture, through manufacturing, into services tracks Rostow's stages from traditional society toward high mass consumption. The recent dominance of services also reflects post-industrial economic restructuring. Topic 7.4. Skill 5.B.

**Q54. B.** Wallerstein's world-systems theory classifies countries as core, semi-periphery, or periphery based on their role in global production, trade, and capital flows, not on climate, religion, or population alone. Topic 7.4. Skill 1.A.

**Q55. A.** HDI combines life expectancy, education (mean and expected years of schooling), and GNI per capita. When a country leads on all three, its HDI score is highest. Topic 7.2. Skill 3.B.

**Q56. A.** GII rises (worsens) with higher maternal mortality, lower female political representation, and lower female labor force participation. Country E is far worse on all three, so its GII score will be substantially higher. Topic 7.2. Skill 3.B.

**Q57. B.** Comparative advantage means producing what you make at relatively lower opportunity cost and trading for the rest. The point is relative efficiency, not absolute self-sufficiency or matching others' export profiles. Topic 7.6. Skill 1.B.

**Q58. B.** The shift from large, vertically integrated factories with stable employment to flexible, contract-based, service-heavy work is the textbook definition of post-Fordist restructuring. Topic 7.7. Skill 1.A.

**Q59. B.** The standard Brundtland-style definition of sustainable development is meeting present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs. Topic 7.8. Skill 1.A.

**Q60. B.** A national HDI of 0.78 hides a high-development northern region, a much lower southern region, and even sharper urban-rural gaps within the south. National-scale data alone misses this, which is the scale-of-analysis lesson. Topic 7.2. Skill 5.A.

## Scoring Rubric (raw score out of 60)

- **50–60 correct: Strong.** Solid command across units. Focus review on any clusters of misses.
- **40–49 correct: Solid.** Real foundation in place, with specific weak spots to clean up.
- **30–39 correct: Gaps to address.** Key concepts and theorists need targeted review before retesting.
- **Below 30 correct: Foundational review.** Work back through the CED unit by unit, with emphasis on definitions, theorists, and scale framing.

## How to Review

After scoring, sort the missed questions two ways:

1. **By unit (and topic code).** Look for clusters. Three misses in Unit 5 with topic codes around 5.10–5.12 means agricultural theory and commodity dependency need targeted review, not a generic Unit 5 reread.
2. **By skill code.** If most misses share a skill code (especially 5.A or 5.B), the issue is how to think about the question, not the content. Scale-of-analysis questions in particular reward practice across many topics, since the framing repeats.

A useful follow-up is to write a one-sentence "what I missed and why" note for each wrong answer. Patterns show up quickly: confusing two theorists, missing a scale-of-analysis cue, misreading a stimulus table, and so on.
